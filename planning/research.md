### Body Swap / Identity Fiction

**Reveal mechanics — types and emotional registers** *(researched session 2026-03-31)*

Five structural types of reveal relevant to a slow-burn, psychologically grounded story:

**The Earned Reveal (Chosen / Gradual)**
The secret-holder decides to disclose after an internal threshold is crossed — the performance is no longer sustainable, or concealment has become a greater betrayal than the truth. Agency fully retained. Emotional register: intimate, heavy, tender or devastating depending on the relationship. Most novelistically satisfying for character-driven work.

**The Slip (Accidental)**
A word, gesture, or reference to something Yooyeon wouldn't know — or a blank where knowledge should be — causes a crack. The secret-holder doesn't choose the moment. Often begins small: one wrong reaction, one too-long pause. The observer may not be sure what they've seen, which extends the tension.

**The Forced Reveal (External Pressure)**
Someone has gathered evidence and confronts directly. The choice is only whether to confess or be exposed. Emotional register: confrontational, often violent in its intimacy. Works well for slow-burn if the confronter has been quietly accumulating evidence for a long time.

**The Witnessed Reveal (Accidental, Third-Party)**
Someone walks in, overhears, or observes behavior that makes the secret legible without the secret-holder knowing they've been seen. Agency fully removed. Power dynamic immediately inverts — the observer holds information Alex doesn't know they have. Creates dramatic irony: subsequent scenes carry different weight for the reader.

**The Partial Reveal / Misread Reveal**
Someone sees or hears something and draws a conclusion — but the wrong one. For a body swap, the most likely first conclusion is not "she's been replaced" but "something is wrong with her." The real reveal happens in two stages: first *something is wrong*, then *she isn't her*. This misreading is load-bearing and should not be skipped — it shapes the emotional texture of the confrontation.

**Key synthesis for this story:**
The most compatible mechanism is behavioral accumulation leading to private confrontation, initiated by the observer rather than the revealer. The observer approaches not with accusation but with concern — "something is wrong with you and I need to know what it is" — forcing Alex to decide whether to confess something that has no credible explanation.

The "Secret Identity Apocalypse" (full public collapse) is the wrong register for this story but is useful as a structural *threat* Alex fears. The gap between "quiet slip that one person notices" and "full collapse in front of the group" is where Arc 4 lives.

Most body swap stories skip the pre-reveal period — the observer's psychology between first suspicion and confrontation. This story should inhabit that space fully.

### Voluntary Disclosure — What Breaks the Secret-Keeper

**From trope research (session 2026-03-31)**

Voluntary disclosure only happens when the cost of concealment exceeds the cost of disclosure. Five trigger types, in order of relevance to this story:

**1. Performance failure → Gravity unit selection (most Alex-appropriate)**
The math changes, not the emotions. After being voted into Visionary Vision, Alex runs the numbers: sustained unit debut, new choreography, promotional cycle, months of compounding pressure. He concludes, alone, that concealment is no longer viable. He chooses to tell someone as a practical decision, not an emotional one. This matches his decision-making pattern exactly.

**2. Member suffering (most emotionally load-bearing — belongs in Arc 4)**
Alex observes, clearly and specifically, that his presence is causing harm to someone — they are quieter, smaller, diminished. He is the cause. Every other trigger is about his own exposure; this one is about someone else's cost. The moment his self-protection stops being worth more than their wellbeing is the fracture. Primary candidate: Seoyeon.

**3. Performance confrontation with Hyerin**
Hyerin has professional authority over him in Visionary Vision and will be the first to see the gap in Yooyeon's dancing formally. A corridor conversation after rehearsal, the others gone — she asks, he gives a non-answer, she doesn't press. *The not-pressing is what stays with him.* Her restraint is more destabilizing than confrontation would be.

**4. Fan sign erosion (cumulative, not a single trigger)**
One-on-one proximity with fans who have calibrated expectations of Yooyeon. Each fan sign is a close-quarters audit. The accumulation of small slips — none decisive alone — builds toward Alex explicitly calculating whether this is sustainable long-term. Not a disclosure trigger in itself; a material that shifts the baseline calculation.

**5. Variety show slip (permanent record fear)**
A member gives Alex a look after he answers wrong about something that involves their private history with Yooyeon. The look, and then the silence, is worse than confrontation. He can't ask what he got wrong. He waits for the consequence.

**How Alex would actually disclose — critical note:**
He won't frame it as unburdening. Won't build to it emotionally. Won't cry. He'll say it flat, minimal, watching for the response. The emotional weight will all be in the other person's face, which he reads and processes without showing. *The gap between what the disclosure means and how he delivers it is where the scene lives.*
