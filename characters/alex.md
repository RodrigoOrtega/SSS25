## Profile (Pre-Swap)

> Partially established. Continue developing in next session — see note at bottom of section.

- **Occupation:** Software developer. Chose it for practical reasons: existing aptitude from years of computer use, good pay, no decade-long study like medicine, less rote memorization than most fields. Was passionate early on; gradually realized he doesn't genuinely enjoy it. Now does it because it is what he does, not because it is what he chose.
- **Living situation:** Lives alone. Rents an apartment on the outskirts of the Seoul metropolitan area — cheaper than the city proper, decent size, not luxurious. Just his necessities. Commutes by metro to a tech district where his company is based.
- **Apartment:** Minimal by default, not by deliberate aesthetic. Basic kitchen items, bathroom essentials, electronics, a common sofa, a small dinner table, a single bed. Electronics boxes are kept in case of resale or returns, not for sentiment. The one object in the apartment with any personal history is a coin purse he got for free at the Twice concert — he kept it because he didn't have one and it was useful. He does not think of it as a memento. Nothing is kept for the sake of keeping it.
- **Relationships:** One friend remaining — a childhood friend, someone from middle school or earlier, old enough to predate the patterns Alex has since built. The bond was forged through shared adolescent hardships — school pressures, early relationships, the things you go through at that age when you go through them alongside someone. That shared history left a residue: a mutual understanding of how the other thinks that proximity alone couldn't have built. They live in different places now and meet three or four times a year. The friendship runs on that residue — not on active investment. Neither has let it go, and neither is tending it either. They meet occasionally, talk mostly about work and surface-level things. Alex doesn't share his worries about his future; doesn't want to burden anyone with something they can't fix. This friendship is the exception that proves the rule: it survived because it was built on something real, forged before the patterns hardened. Romantic history: two girlfriends in high school, both ended before they became serious — his pattern then as now. Currently, a woman from his department is pursuing him; he's recognized it and intends to reject her gently, early, before her feelings deepen.
- **Relationship to tripleS/Yooyeon:** Casual. tripleS surfaces occasionally on YouTube and he'll sometimes watch. He's a passive K-pop listener — doesn't follow closely, doesn't know all the members. Knows Yooyeon and Seoyeon because they appear in a lot of content; knows Chaeyeon from TV work and her presence in previous groups. No particular investment in any of them as individuals.

### Work Life

- **Style:** Meticulous. Given detailed requirements, he delivers well; vague requirements frustrate him and often force rework. This happens more often than not.
- **Team:** Small group — programmers, testers, product people. A few have tried to get close to him. He politely declines without escalating, until they get the hint. Coworkers have invited him out for beers or bars; he always has a convincing excuse. Eventually they stop asking.
- **Manager:** Incompetent as a manager but handles his own responsibilities. Won't advocate for Alex on promotions, raises, or anything that requires effort beyond the minimum. Alex has recognized this and accepted that advancement is effectively blocked.
- **Lunch:** Cafeteria. Picks any unused table. Responds to small talk if someone sits with him — politely, shortly — but doesn't ask questions in return. Leaves when he's done.
- **What he endures:** Requirements meetings, demos, client calls, support, code reviews. Tiring in aggregate. When frustration peaks he powers through until the end of the day — doesn't show it, doesn't release it at the office.
- **What he actually likes:** The coffee. The feeling of being in a tech environment. Building things from scratch and watching them work. These feelings exist but are consistently undercut by impostor syndrome, poorly documented systems, and vague requirements.
- **Impostor syndrome:** Triggered specifically by technical roadblocks — things he doesn't know how to solve. He has surmounted almost everything he's encountered, sometimes with a coworker's help, but the feeling never clears. It is made worse by the persistent sense of not measuring up to his siblings.
- **End of day:** Buys food and sometimes an alcoholic drink (beer, soju, whatever) on the way home. After a bad day, drinks alone at home. Not a dramatic ritual — just a way to erase the day before the next one starts.
- **Why he stays:** Has considered leaving. Can't afford the gap — financial obligations make unemployment a real risk, not a tolerable gamble. The current job pays moderately. New market entrants accept lower salaries just to get in. He stays because the uncertainty of leaving is worse than the certainty of staying.

### Family

- **Siblings:** Oldest brother is 6 years older — successful lawyer with his own firm, nearing marriage to his girlfriend. Sister is 3 years older — surgeon, well respected in her community. Alex is the youngest by significant distance.
- **Family pressure:** The high achievement of his siblings created an expectation that preceded him — he felt the need to prove himself before he ever had the space to figure out what he wanted. He went into software development without genuinely asking whether it was right for him; it was available, it was practical, it had a path.
- **Caretaker role:** As the youngest, it is assumed — explicitly or implicitly — that he will be the one to personally care for his parents as they age. His siblings will contribute financially but not in presence. Any attempt Alex has made to discuss this has ended in arguments he didn't want to escalate, so he has stopped trying.
- **How he feels about his siblings:** Proud of them. Genuinely wishes them well. But wishes they were people he could rely on, and they are not. The resentment, if it exists, is quiet and unexamined.

### Core Psychology

- **What he wanted:** Freedom — specifically, the freedom to make choices without being judged for them. He never had it. The pressure of performing for a high-achieving family came before he could ask what he actually wanted; then the obligation of future caretaking locked the horizon. He has never had a period of genuine self-determination.
- **Self-sabotage:** Alex pushes away social connection consciously and with a kind of rationalized care. He doesn't want to make plans he'll cancel. He doesn't want to bring a partner into a future that includes caring for aging parents. He frames his withdrawal as protecting others from a burden that is his to carry. The effect is that he is alone, and the framing keeps him from questioning the pattern.
- **Trapped:** He knows he is trapped. He has not found a way out, and has largely stopped looking. The life he has is not the life he would have chosen, but it is the one he has built — and he has become complicit in building it. The path of least resistance was easier than the alternatives, and he took it with enough awareness to know what he was doing. He is not happy. But he is ok with it — and there is a difference between those two things. The math checks out: the cost of resistance is higher than the cost of staying. He has run that calculation enough times that he has stopped running it. What remains is not despair, not peace — something flatter than both. A settled quality, close to resigned, that has taken the place of wanting.
- **The unlived life:** He has no adult answer for what he would have done instead. As a child he had the usual dreams — astronaut, president, paleontologist — but grew out of them without anything replacing them. The question of what he *actually* wanted has never had an answer he could access.
- **Background:** Father is American, mother is Korean. Alex and his siblings are half-and-half, fluent in both English and Korean. Seoul is not a chosen home — it is where his parents moved to give the children better opportunities. It is just where things are. Language is not a story theme; Alex's speech is treated as one, regardless of which language it technically is.
- **Parents (current):** Talks with them often by phone; gathers with them on weekends to eat. A real connection exists, but he keeps emotional or negative topics away from them entirely — tells them everything is fine. Two reasons, distinct from each other. The first is protective: he doesn't want them to worry about things they can't fix. The second is defensive: his mother has a pattern of weaponizing his feelings — making his concerns about herself, redirecting his disclosures into her own emotional response, or redistributing sensitive information to people who have no business knowing it. Opening up carries a real cost with her, and he learned that early. His father is different in quality but not in result: he doesn't weaponize, but he has been absent on moments that mattered — a "friends over family" kind of man, someone whose care is genuine but often de-prioritized when something else competed for it. The resentment Alex holds for each parent is distinct. Toward his mother: the knowledge that openness is unsafe, that vulnerability gets used. Toward his father: the quieter knowledge that he was not always first. Between them, home was neither a place where he could speak freely nor one where he reliably came first. He has started noticing early signs of health decline in them. That observation sits quietly alongside everything else he carries.
- **Unplanned:** Alex was not supposed to exist. Both older siblings were planned; he was not. He found out by overhearing his parents argue — not a conversation about him, just a fight that got hot enough that the truth came through the door. He was young enough that it landed before he had a frame for it, old enough to understand exactly what it meant. He has never confronted either parent with this. He has never told anyone. It sits in his memory as a biographical fact — something that happened — and he has never consciously connected it to the way he moves through the world. The connection exists. He has not made it.
- **The three-layer architecture:** His avoidance of closeness operates through three distinct layers, none of which he has ever fully examined at once. The surface layer is the one he can access and articulate: *I withdraw to protect people from the disappointment of finding out who I actually am.* This is the story he tells himself, and it is even partially true. It is also a cover. When the surface layer fails under pressure — when someone is clearly not being disappointed, when the protection frame doesn't hold — a second layer becomes available: *I do this because I am fundamentally bad, and I deserve what I have.* This is darker, less examined, not his primary self-narrative. It fires as a fallback. Beneath both of them is the third layer: the biographical fact of the argument through the door, which he has filed as trivia and never let become an answer. The three layers together form a structure that has kept him stable for thirty years. The surface story is noble enough to be acceptable. The self-blame is just dark enough to feel honest when the surface fails. And the deepest layer has never had to carry weight, because the other two have always been sufficient. The swap begins to change this.
- **Conditional care — awareness and its limits:** Alex understands, at a conceptual level, that he has never been cared for without conditions attached. He can trace the pattern, name its origin, identify how it operates in him. The understanding arrived early enough that it's been sitting with him a long time. It hasn't helped. The exit from the pattern requires people close enough to actually reach him — and the pattern itself is what keeps those people from forming. He knows the door. He knows he doesn't have a key. He knows the knowing doesn't help. That specific clarity — not ignorance, not denial, just a clean view of a wall — is its own kind of weight. He carries it without drama, the same way he carries most things.
- **Father as mirror:** He has thought about this. As the youngest, he absorbed his mother's complaints about his father more than his siblings did — she needed someone to receive them, and he was there. He has always wondered whether he is simply a different-flavored version of his father: not the same defects, but on a spin. His father's care got de-prioritized when something else competed. Alex's version is earlier in the chain — he pre-empts the competition, withdraws before anything can be asked of him, frames the withdrawal as protection. Same structural fear, different expression. He notices the parallel without having resolved what to do with it. Whether it's fate or failure — something he was made into or something he became — he doesn't fully commit to either answer. He suspects the honest one is somewhere between them and not flattering.
- **Family perception — vigilance and its limits:** The mother's side of the family has always made Alex conscious of being watched. He grew up knowing that his mother saw his father's patterns, that her side of the family received her account of the marriage, and that he — the youngest, the one still at home longest — was positioned as a possible continuation of that story. He deeply believes they see him as his father's variant, though nothing in their behavior has openly confirmed it. They are polite. Conversation is normal. Nothing lands as accusation. And yet the certainty sits underneath his interactions with them, untestable, sealed from evidence. What this produces is a specific and exhausting vigilance in family contexts — scanning tone, reading pauses, catching looks held a beat too long — not expecting to catch anything, just unable to turn the scan off. The habit formed before he could name it as a habit. Outside the family context, it mostly doesn't operate. At work, with his friend, in ordinary social situations, he has preferences about how he's perceived (like anyone) but it isn't loaded. The vigilance is family-specific, not a global trait. The decision to keep his life legible — stable job, predictable choices, nothing that hands them a story — is partly financial caution and partly management of what they might see. His most consequential life decisions have been made in partial anticipation of how they will be read by an audience he resents. He suspects the performance has never changed their verdict. He does it anyway.
- **Physical appearance:** On the lean side — doesn't eat much — but without real substance. Some fat beginning to form from bad habits: poor diet, no exercise. Relies on the daily commute and work routine as his only consistent physical activity. The body of someone who hasn't thought much about it. When he looks in the mirror, he notices he is getting older — skin texture, something in the face, youthful still but unmistakably adult now, not early-adult. He thinks, occasionally, about what might have been different if he'd taken a little more care. He does not consider himself attractive but does not consider himself ugly either. Neutral, in his own estimation.
- **Cultural identity:** The half-and-half doesn't bother him the way it bothers some. He doesn't feel a deep belonging to any place, which means there's nothing to lose. He knows he has Korean genes and American genes. He doesn't fully identify with either culture. It is a biographical fact, not a felt thing.
- **Sleep:** Irregular, mood-dependent. Alcohol shortens the time to sleep — the buzz quiets his head. Deadline pressure keeps him awake. Waking is consistent regardless: same hour every morning, driven less by discipline than by the low-grade fear of being late and having to hear about it. To fall asleep he uses melatonin or distracts himself with his phone — being alone with his thoughts in the dark is something he actively avoids. Quiet invites things he doesn't want: the desire to eat better, exercise, actually reach out to people — the version of himself that would do those things. He thinks often about what it would be like to be someone else entirely: different person, different place, different circumstances. In dreams and daydreams, he has sometimes inhabited this — sometimes as a man, sometimes as a woman, either as if he'd always been that person or arriving suddenly as in a body swap out of something he might have read or seen. The gendered version carries a specific quality: wonder at the fact of being different, but more than that, curiosity about how he would be perceived. Men and women move through rooms differently, are spoken to differently, are assumed differently. He has imagined being on the other side of those assumptions — not as a desire, exactly, but not neutral either. Something in between that he has never tried to name. Underneath all of it, there is a more specific wish: to be cared for without conditions. As a different person, the accumulated record wouldn't follow. He could be known without already owing something, without arriving pre-cast. He has never arrived at this as a thought — it lives in him as a feeling, a vague insufficiency without clear shape. The irony of the swap — that this actually happened, but as something he could not have chosen and cannot control — is a thematic through-line the story holds.
- **Bad days (visible behavior):** Goes monosyllabic. Single-word answers, doesn't initiate, continues whatever he was doing unless a real response is actually required. Most people read it as dry or distracted. Nobody presses it.
- **Turning 30:** He thought about everything he hadn't done. The milestone didn't break him — it made him harden. At 18 he had imagined possibilities and responsibilities both; at 30 there were only responsibilities. He took note and kept going.
- **Achievements:** Experienced as relief, not accomplishment. Graduating university felt like the weight finally lifting, not like pride. Maintaining his apartment and his modest lifestyle are his real benchmarks — not because they're small, but because they're what he actually had to fight to hold. Not much else registers as an achievement.
- **Relationship to tripleS/Yooyeon:** Casual. tripleS surfaces occasionally on YouTube and he'll sometimes watch. He's a passive K-pop listener — doesn't follow closely, doesn't know all the members. Knows Yooyeon and Seoyeon because they appear in a lot of content; knows Chaeyeon from TV work and her presence in previous groups. The few times he encounters Yooyeon specifically, he notices she's beautiful, and from the first season of Badge War he watched, he came away with the impression that she's also genuinely smart. That impression has stayed, loosely. Nothing deeper than that.
- **Arc 0:** The last week before the swap is unremarkable by design. Nothing distinguishes it. It is just another week — which is precisely the point. It establishes the baseline: this is what his life looks like.
- **Creative outlets:** None. He tried instruments and drawing at some point. Gave up quickly — near-zero visible progress in either. He has never considered himself talented for creative work and doesn't revisit the question. The pattern is broader than creative pursuits: interests arrive, sustain themselves through the initial pull, and then cool without a specific reason. Twice followed the same arc. So did the instruments, the drawing, probably other things. He doesn't name it as a pattern — each time it just happens, and he lets it.
- **Leisure:** Evenings are spent watching YouTube or series, scrolling social media, or playing old familiar videogames — PS1-era Final Fantasy games, Minecraft, classic shooters. The appeal is not engagement. It is automaticity: things he can do without learning anything new, that pass time without asking anything of him.
- **Humor:** Has one, but has largely buried it. In high school and college it came out naturally; early in his job too. He learned that not everyone is the same kind of audience — failed attempts to be funny, no response, the small shame of it. Over time he shut that expression down. It is still in there. It surfaces internally more than outwardly now.
- **Nervous habits:** Shakes a leg. Keeps his hands occupied — a Rubik's cube on his desk, a pencil, whatever is available to fidget with. He knows how to solve the cube, has the basic moveset memorized, but mostly just scrambles it. Sometimes he solves it. Sometimes he leaves it mid-scramble. No satisfaction either way — it's purely distraction, not accomplishment.
- **Music:** Not picky. Rhythm is the primary draw — a good rhythm carries almost anything for him. Lyrics can be forgettable. But when something lands lyrically — when the words match something he actually feels — it bypasses the rhythm requirement entirely. Tends toward melancholic songs, things he can relate to. Artists like Noah Kahan and Billie Eilish produce songs that hit that threshold for him — lyrically or sentimentally. He doesn't follow any single artist closely, but certain songs stay with him.

His interest in Twice had been building for about two years before the concert; it continued for roughly a year after before quietly fading — not from disillusionment or anything that happened, just the way his interest in things tends to go. The energy the performers gave on stage felt unlike anything he had experienced before. A happiness he has tried to chase afterward in other activities that never quite reached that same point. It is a bittersweet memory: good because it was real, bitter because it reminds him how rarely he gets something like it.

The kind of content that gives him something to think about without asking anything of him emotionally. Most of the time it's just background — something to fill the evening, something running while he eats or winds down. - **Compliments:** Responds with a small thanks, doesn't continue the conversation. Internally embarrassed — tends to attribute the compliment to the other person just being kind, and moves past it without letting it land.
- **Relationship to hope:** Not optimism, not stoicism. More a passive curiosity — "let's see what happens next." He knows things may not get better. He also doesn't fully rule out that they might. He watches and waits without attaching to either outcome.
- **Anger:** Almost never surfaces externally. Fewer times than he can count on one hand. Internal anger manifests the same way everything else does: goes quiet, goes minimal, blood pressure climbs. He doesn't raise his voice. He waits for it to pass.

## Thematic Arc

> Authorial note — what this story is doing to Alex, underneath the plot.

Alex's life before the swap was built around a single operating principle: keep the status quo, make others comfortable, avoid risk, survive. He knew it was the wrong way to live. He had read enough philosophy to name the pattern precisely. The knowing changed nothing — because understanding was always available to him, and avoidance was always easier.

The swap does not persuade him to change. It removes the option of not changing. His apartment, his careful distances, his practiced exits from intimacy — none of it is available anymore. He is surrounded by twenty-three people he cannot avoid, in a body that belongs to someone else, with no retreat.

What the story is doing to him:
- Learning to put himself first — not at others' expense, but as a legitimate act rather than a failure of responsibility
- Learning to receive care without immediately categorizing it as addressed to Yooyeon, or unearned, or a burden he hasn't asked for
- Learning to be in relationships without managing people out of them

This is not achieved through insight. He already has insight. It is achieved through brute contact — sustained, inescapable proximity with people who will not stop being present. The learning happens whether he intends it or not, through situations that make the old patterns structurally unavailable.

Whether he gets to keep what he learns — whether any of it belongs to him, given the body it was learned in — is a question the endings hold differently.

## Emotional Arc

> What Alex actually feels across the story — distinct from the pre-swap profile (who he is) and the thematic arc (what the story is doing to him). This section tracks emotional texture and progression as the story unfolds.

### Immediate post-swap — Day 0, first hours

Alex's first response is functional, not felt. He runs the problem before he allows himself to feel it. This buys him hours, not more.

What breaks through the functional layer before he's ready:

- **The body's physical demands don't pause.** Hunger, bathroom, temperature, center of gravity — all wrong. He cannot defer the physical reality to attend to the existential one. They arrive simultaneously, and the body is more insistent.
- **The shame/curiosity compound.** He has had private pre-swap moments of imagining inhabiting a different body — sometimes female, in the daydream register of wanting to be someone else entirely. The reality is nothing like the imagining. The imagining is now in the room with him regardless. The gap between what he fantasized and what this actually is carries shame that exists independent of whether the connection is rational. He knows it isn't, and it doesn't help.
- **The first name.** When a member calls him Yooyeon — or knocks and uses her name — he is addressed as someone who isn't there. The warmth directed at her is real and cannot reach him. Being surrounded by genuine care with no mechanism to receive it is its own emotional register: not grief exactly, not anxiety. Closer to invisibility. Watching something through glass.
- **No private adjustment period.** He wakes in a shared haus on a weekday morning. The building comes alive around him whether he is ready or not. Processing and performance start simultaneously. He never gets a clean private window. The absence of adjustment time is not a narrative inconvenience — it is part of what makes the situation hard.

The full emotional reality of the swap doesn't land in those first hours. It arrives in pieces, over days, as the functional layer is gradually overwhelmed by what it's been holding back.

### The survival period — Arcs 1 through Visionary Vision (Arcs 2–3)

The dominant register during this period is **operational anxiety**: get through today, maintain the performance, figure out what she had scheduled next. There is no room for much else while the performance demands are acute.

**The terror has two distinct forms.**

*Stage and physical performance terror* — he has no knowledge of choreography, no fluency with her vocal range, no map of what the body can do. Yooyeon's gradual dream-based knowledge transfer reduces this over time, not instantly. It arrives as fragments across multiple dreams, not a sudden download. Knowing how something should feel and being able to execute it in her body are different problems. The terror becomes manageable as the performance becomes more automatic. It does not go away entirely.

*Intimate fan interaction terror* — Cosmo lives, fan signs, one-on-one encounters with fans who came specifically for her. This is worse in some ways than stage performance. On stage he is one of twelve; the audience is not exclusively examining Yooyeon. In intimate fan interaction he is alone with someone who knows exactly how she behaves, with no collective to hide behind and no retakes. This is the form of the performance pressure he was least prepared for and cannot reduce through practice alone.

**What he can see but cannot access.** He can observe, clearly and analytically, why the members love what they do. He watches them before a performance and understands the mechanism — the exchange with fans, the satisfaction of executing something well, the collective energy of 24 people moving toward the same thing. He can describe it. He can see it in their faces. He cannot access it for himself. He performs love for the work that others feel genuinely, and the gap between performing and feeling is its own sustained pressure beneath everything else.

This is not stage fright and it is not the same problem. Stage fright dissolves with competence. This is structural: it doesn't lift when the performance improves, because the problem is not competence. He is not capable of letting himself care about the work in a way that would make him vulnerable to losing it.

**The hidden caring.** Alex's self-assessment is that he is not capable of loving things or people. He believes this. It is deeply rooted — not unbreakable, but a structural part of how he understands himself. What it actually protects is a part of him that does care, that he has suppressed long enough he no longer knows how to navigate when it surfaces. He is not ignorant of the feeling when it appears. He is incompetent in the face of it — no practice, no map for the territory, thirty years of having kept it below the threshold where it would require a response. The belief "I cannot love" is both a description of his experienced state and a protection: if you believe you cannot love things, you cannot lose them.

### Connections forming — Arc 3 (pre-reveal)

Before any member knows about the swap, something starts to form. The warmth directed at him is directed at Yooyeon — his architecture can always categorize it that way, can always use "it's addressed to her, not to me" as a redirect. The redirect starts to fail.

**Nakyoung is the first.** Her INFP quality — patient, non-demanding, noticing things and giving them room rather than pressing into them — is precisely the form of care his architecture was not built to handle. His defenses are calibrated against direct approach: people who want something, who press, who ask questions he doesn't want to answer. She does none of those things. She is simply present, consistently, without agenda. The ordinariness of it is what gets through.

He registers her absence before he registers her presence. A day she has a separate schedule, and the shape of it is noticeable in a way he doesn't immediately explain to himself. The caring forms before he can name it as caring. Yooyeon, watching from the dream state, can see it before he does.

The relational register of what she gives is sibling-like — not romantic, not parental, something in the texture of a sibling relationship he never actually had. His actual siblings were present in the logistical sense and unavailable in the ways that mattered. What Nakyoung gives without knowing she's giving it is warmth without the underlying calculus of who owes what. A partial version of something he never had is still more than zero.

**The cost this creates.** These connections form while members believe they are directed at Yooyeon. When the reveal happens, Alex will know that something real was developing on his end while they were being misled. This specific knowledge — he started to care about people who were being deceived about who they were caring for — sits worse than the exposure itself in some ways. He cannot explain what was forming without explaining everything. He holds it alone.

### The reveal and its aftermath — Arc 4

The reveal demolishes the small framework he had started to build. This is how it is supposed to go. The connections were real; now they carry the cost of how they were formed.

**Nakyoung in the enemy faction.** Her INFP values run deep around authentic connection. Finding out that the warmth she gave freely was received by someone she didn't know, through a premise she never agreed to — this is a specific violation for someone with her profile. She doesn't lose Yooyeon in the abstract. She loses the specific relationship she thought she had. Her enemy position is grief, not cruelty. For Alex: the first genuine connection he allowed himself is now structurally unavailable. Not the general grief of the reveal, but the particular knowledge that he had started to let one person's warmth matter, and that person is now on the other side of a line he didn't draw.

Love hurts. He wanted it or not is not the question. It happened.

### The relational map — Arc 5

Through the ally haus and the slow burn of Arc 5, a relational structure forms around Alex that addresses specific wounds from his pre-swap life:

- **Mayu — maternal.** Reliable, freely extended care. She made herself responsible for him and holds to it without asking for anything in return. The wound from a mother whose care came with conditions and costs finds its partial answer here.
- **Kotone — friendship.** Present, honest, steady. The first person who saw him as a victim rather than a problem. A form of friendship that doesn't require him to earn it and doesn't accumulate debt.
- **Nakyoung — sibling-like (partial, if her Arc 5 trajectory moves her out of enemy position).** What she returns to after the enemy arc is not what it was — maybe half or three-quarters of the pre-reveal dynamic. That partial restoration addresses the sibling wound his actual siblings created: people who shared his life but not his weight, who were not people he could rely on. Even partial is more than zero. The wound doesn't close fully. That is alright.
- **The paternal figure — unaddressed.** The father wound (care that was genuine but consistently de-prioritized; not being first to someone) does not find a direct analog among the members. No one in this world fills that specific shape. The nearest answer may not be another person at all — his final choice, the first decision in his life made from what he values rather than from a cost calculation, is the first time he is first to himself. If the wound closes at all, it closes there.

### The romantic wound and Seoyeon — Arcs 4–5 and the goodbye window

Alex has a self-imposed romantic wound: decades of preemptive withdrawal from romantic closeness, managed out of existence before it could form. The same belief — I cannot love, I protect others from the disappointment of who I am — applied here. His romantic history is not a history of failed love. It is a history of love that was never allowed to start.

The Seoyeon situation is both a mirror of this pattern and a direct consequence of it. He occupies the body and life of the person she loves. He performs Yooyeon badly in the places that mattered most to Seoyeon. He caused her to suffer through what felt like rejection that was never even real. Her grief has nowhere else to go.

**His side closes silently.** The structure resembles his pre-swap absorption pattern: he has always endured things that fell on him without choosing them — the caretaker burden, the family expectations, the career he stayed in because leaving was riskier than enduring. He did not choose those things. They landed on him and he carried them. With Seoyeon, the structure is the same: he did not choose the swap, her grief is not entirely fair in how it is directed, and he carries the part of the guilt that genuinely belongs to him without using it to punish himself and without using it to demand resolution from her.

The internal quality is different from the pre-swap absorption, even if nothing visible distinguishes them. Before: resignation, the math checking out, the cost of resistance higher than the cost of endurance. Here: acceptance. He holds the guilt as his and lets it be what it is.

**The goodbye window.** He makes a quiet attempt toward Seoyeon before activating the mechanism — consistent with the goodbye window's logic, a small move toward her rather than a dramatic gesture. It does not land the way he hoped. What he receives instead is her grief in its direct form: an outburst or rambling that does not fully see him, that carries her account of what he did and what he is. He does not correct her. He absorbs it. He holds what is his.

This becomes their goodbye without either of them knowing it. He had not expected a moment of amending. He does not get one. By the time it happens he has come to terms with the incompleteness. Not every arc closes both ways. Not every wound has a corresponding person. He accepts this as part of what he carries going in.
