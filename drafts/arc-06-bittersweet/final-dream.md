# Draft — The Final Dream
## Arc 6, Bittersweet Ending
## Status: First draft

> **Context for this scene:** This is the last of a recurring sequence of dreams that Alex has had since Arc 2. In all previous dreams, the pattern was the same: she was present but not reachable, visible but unclear, the gap between them never closing no matter how he moved toward her. He learned, eventually, to wait rather than reach. This scene is different. The gap closes — from her side. It is the first and only time.
>
> After this scene: the dreams don't return. The ending belongs to Alex alone.

---

The dreams had always had the same quality. Not darkness exactly — something more like the space between one room and another, as if the world had paused mid-breath and he was standing in the gap. She was always there. Not close. But there.

He had learned, over however long it had been, not to run toward her. Running in the dream didn't close distance; it moved him sideways, or not at all, or brought him somewhere different. He had learned to wait. To let the space between them do whatever it was going to do.

Tonight it did something new.

The gap closed.

Not because he moved toward her. Not because he reached. She came toward him — or the space compressed — or something that had always held the distance finally didn't, and then she was close enough that he could see her face clearly for the first time, and she looked exactly like the face he'd been looking at in the mirror for years. Exactly like the face in the photographs, the videos, the files he'd gone through in those first weeks in her apartment. It was strange to see yourself approaching yourself. He had no frame for what he was looking at.

She said: *I have somewhere, some place to go.*

Her voice was the voice he had been using. Of course it was. He knew that, technically. He had known it the whole time. But hearing it come from her, from the direction of someone else, from outside —

He reached.

Both hands. Across the last distance. He knew what he was doing and he did it anyway, which was becoming, he had noticed, a pattern.

*Don't,* he thought, or tried to say. *Don't. I'll go. Tell me how and I'll go. This doesn't have to be — there has to be —*

He didn't know if she could hear him. He had never known.

He thought she could. He thought she could hear him.

She looked at him the way you look at something you've been watching for a long time. Not studying. Not judging. The way you look at something when you've already decided.

*And you do too,* she said.

He was still reaching.

Then he wasn't reaching for anything.

---

He woke up before it was light. Yooyeon's body. His ceiling. Sounds from the floor above — someone moving through the haus, early, the building settling into morning.

He lay there for a while.

He had learned something across these years that he hadn't known before: that grief doesn't always announce itself. Sometimes it arrives as stillness, as the absence of something you didn't know you'd been bracing for. He lay there and felt the shape of what she'd left him with, and he couldn't tell yet — it was too new, too unformed — whether it was the saddest thing that had ever happened to him.

*You do too.*

He had somewhere to go.

He didn't know what to do with that yet. He supposed he'd find out.
